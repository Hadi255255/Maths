Who is this site for?

This site is designed to teach primary school students to the second, third and fourth grades, the multiplication and division tables and other, and to help the student understand and memorize the multiplication tables.
Parents can see the site well to know all the tools on the site and take full advantage of it.

How do I use the site?

① Have a look at the site's tools first and read the information in it and also in the 'Help' section, Then study the information in this section to learn the meaning of the multiplication process before starting to memorize, then you can start by testing the first table or two tables: one and two of the multiplication tables.
② Do not move to memorizing a new table before memorizing the tables that precede it well and without any error.
③ You can go to test the division tables only for the numbers you learned in the multiplication tables and After studying the explanation here about division, to understand it better. And to learn the meanings of the division process.
④ After mastering the tables you learned, you can move on to learning the general exams and test yourself in it.
⑤ In the tests there is a timer that you can control its speed on the left of the screen. Don't move up to a faster speed until you've mastered the previous speed.
The ideal speed is the second and the third is for the challenge and it is not necessary for the student to practice it.


Explanation the multiplication process

The multiplication process (or product) means the repetition, or: the number of repetitions.
For example: 2 x 4 means 4 twice or 2 four times, choose whichever is easier.
So: 4 × 2 = 4 + 4.
Or: 4 × 2 = 2 + 2 + 2 + 2.
In both cases, the answer is 8. One may be easier than the other, for example:
10 × 2 = 20
10 + 10 = 20
Easier than: 2 + 2 + 2 + 2 + 2 + 2 + 2 + 2 + 2 = 20.

Note 1:
Multiplication is a commutative operation, that is:
3 × 5 = 5 × 3
4 × 6 = 6 × 4
2 × 8 = 8 × 2

Note 2:
The student must memorize only one table at a time and not move to memorizing another table until he has memorized the one before it in an excellent manner .

Note 3:
After memorizing tables from one to five, learn how to count with fingers for numbers greater than five, this rule is explained after explaining the table number five.

The method of counting with fingers for numbers greater than five:

And it is an easy method for the student that he can use easily after learning it until he memorizes all the multiplication tables.

First example: 8 × 9
We put in one hand the number of fingers of the product of subtraction: 10 - 9 = 1
On the other hand, the result of subtracting 10 - 8 = 2
Thus we will open one finger on the first hand and two fingers on the other hand.

Then we multiply 1 × 2
1: Represents the number of outstretched fingers on the first hand
2: Represents the number of outstretched fingers on the second hand
2 × 1 = 2
Then we gather the fingers that are not extended in the hands together, but ten ten, that is, each finger represents ten, and since they are 7 fingers The result is: 70. The result of multiplication is: 70 + 2 = 72.

Second example: 8 × 6
10 - 8 = 2
10 - 6 = 4
On the first hand: two fingers
On the second hand there are 4 fingers

4 × 2 = 8
The number of unstretched fingers is 4 each of ten, totaling: 40
Final result: 40 + 8 = 48
There are two cases that we recommend memorizing instead of calculating them with fingers, because calculating them may be more difficult for the student than memorizing them, and They:
6 × 6 = 36
6 × 7 = 42

Third example:Finger counting method for 6 × 6:

Multiplying the number of outstretched fingers: 4 × 4 = 16
The number of unstretched fingers is 2 each of ten, totaling: 20

Fourth example:Finger counting method for 7 × 6:

Explanation of the division process

There are three methods for the division process: distribution, quantity, and inverse multiplication.
We use one of the first or second methods, whichever is easier, at the beginning of learning the division process to make it easier for the student understanding The division process is good.
① Distribution:
Example: 8 ÷ 2 = ?
Eight things, let them be pens, for example, we want to distribute them equally to two people, for example, how many pens are we give it to everyone one of them?
The answer is 4, because 4 + 4 = 8.
② Quantity:
Example: 6 ÷ 3 = ?
How many three are in the number six?
Answer: 2.
Note:One of the two methods may be easier than the other, as per the example:
For example: 20 ÷ 2
The way to distribute here is easier: 20 apples we distribute to two people equally, how many apples does each take? The answer is 10
While: 20 ÷ 10
The quantum method here is easier: How many ten are there in the number twenty? Answer: 2
③ Reverse multiplication:
Here the student must have memorized the multiplication tables for the numbers in the problems.
And this is the method that the student will rely on after understanding the meaning of division in the two previous ways, because he cannot use them only for easy cases.
Example: 30 ÷ 6 = ?
What is the number that if we multiply it by the divisor (ie 6) the result is the number that we divide (ie 30)?
Here the student should remember from the multiplication tables:
5 × 6 = 30
At first, the student may find it difficult to remember because it is a search process that is going on in his mind now about two numbers that are 30 and one of them is 6.
And in case he does not remember directly, he can refer to Table No. 6 in his mind in this example:
He says:
6 × 1 = 6 .........It is not the output we are looking for
6 × 2 = 12 .......It is not the output we are looking for
6 × 3 = 18 .......It is not the output we are looking for
6 × 4 = 24 .......It is not the output we are looking for
6 × 5 = 30 .......And it is the result we are looking for, so the answer is 5, meaning that:
30 ÷ 6 = 5

Note 1:Division is not commutative.
That is, 9 ÷ 3 does not equal 3 ÷ 9.

Note 2:
Every multiplication of two numbers results in two divisions
They are: the product of the multiplication process, if we divide it by one of the two numbers, the result is the other number , for example:
6 × 5 = 30
Result:
30 ÷ 6 = 5
30 ÷ 5 = 6

Explanation of the general exams

I chose one type of problem that is difficult for students at this stage, namely:
Two numbers whose sum is known and their product is known, so what are they?
In order for the student to get used to this type of problem, the student can know one of the two numbers, which is the table he chose.
If he chooses more than one table, then one of the two numbers is from one of the tables he chose.

Example:
Two numbers multiplied by 24 and their sum is 10, so what are they?
The student looks in his mind at the multiplication tables and remembers that:
8 × 3 = 24
6 × 4 = 24
The sum of the two numbers in the first case is 11 contrary to what is required, so they are not the answer.
The sum of the two numbers in the second case is 10, which is required.
So the two numbers are: 6, 4.

Note:
This type of problem is solved in this elementary stage by remembering and experimenting, meaning that he tries two numbers each time and he sees the result of multiplying them, then if it is what is required, he tries what is the product of their sum.
